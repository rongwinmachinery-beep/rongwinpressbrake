## Introduction

Choosing a press brake is not only a machine purchase. For most sheet metal factories, it is a decision that affects production speed, part accuracy, operator workload, tooling cost, energy use, delivery planning, and after-sales service for years. A low-price machine that cannot bend the required material, hold repeatable angles, or receive spare parts quickly can become more expensive than a better-specified machine from the beginning.

This guide is written for overseas buyers, importers, dealers, distributors, contractors, EPC companies, and fabrication workshops that need a practical way to evaluate a press brake before sending an RFQ. It focuses on the questions RONGWIN engineers commonly need before recommending a machine: material, thickness, bending length, part type, tonnage, tooling, controller, backgauge, production volume, destination country, installation preparation, and inspection requirements.

The goal is not to push one model for every buyer. A small cabinet workshop, a construction parts supplier, and a heavy fabrication company may all need different bending solutions. The best press brake is the one that fits your workpieces, operators, factory layout, service expectations, and long-term production plan.

## 1. Start with the Parts You Need to Bend

Many purchasing mistakes happen because the buyer starts with a machine model instead of the parts. A press brake should be selected around real production requirements. Before asking for a quotation, prepare several sample drawings or at least a simple list of the parts you want to produce.

Useful information includes:

- Material type, such as carbon steel, stainless steel, aluminum, or galvanized sheet.
- Maximum and common material thickness.
- Maximum bending length.
- Typical flange height and part shape.
- Whether the parts are simple angles, boxes, cabinets, channels, panels, or complex multi-bend components.
- Required accuracy and repeatability.
- Daily or monthly production volume.
- Whether the production is prototype, batch, or continuous manufacturing.

For example, a factory bending 1.5 mm stainless steel cabinet doors has different requirements from a workshop bending 8 mm carbon steel structural parts. Both may need a press brake, but the tonnage, tooling, backgauge, controller, and handling method can be very different.

## 2. Understand Press Brake Tonnage

Tonnage is the bending force of the press brake. It is affected by material tensile strength, thickness, bending length, die opening, and bend angle. Thicker material, longer bending length, and smaller V-die openings require more tonnage.

A practical buyer should not choose tonnage only by “bigger is safer.” Oversizing can increase purchase cost, energy consumption, tooling requirements, and factory space. Undersizing can cause machine overload, poor bending quality, slow production, or inability to process future orders.

When discussing tonnage with a supplier, provide:

- Maximum material thickness.
- Maximum bending length.
- Material grade if known.
- Required inside radius.
- Die opening if already specified.
- Whether the machine must handle occasional heavy-duty jobs.

If you are unsure, ask the supplier to calculate a recommended tonnage range and explain the assumption. A responsible press brake manufacturer should not only quote a model number; they should explain why the machine fits the material and parts.

## 3. Confirm Bending Length and Working Width

Bending length is another critical parameter. A 3200 mm press brake may be suitable for many general sheet metal workshops, while longer machines may be needed for door panels, construction profiles, large cabinets, or special enclosures. However, a longer machine also affects floor space, shipping, foundation planning, and operator handling.

When choosing bending length, consider:

- The longest part you need to bend today.
- Possible future orders.
- Whether the full length is frequently used or only occasional.
- How operators will load and support long sheets.
- Whether front support arms or sheet followers are needed.

For long or heavy workpieces, handling becomes as important as bending capacity. If the machine has enough tonnage but the operator cannot safely position the sheet, production will still be inefficient. For export projects, it is also useful to ask the supplier how the machine will be packed, loaded, and shipped.

## 4. Choose the Right Control System

The controller determines how operators program bends, manage angles, store jobs, and reduce setup time. A basic controller may be enough for simple repeat work. A more advanced CNC controller is better when the factory has multiple part types, frequent job changes, complex bend sequences, or accuracy requirements.

When evaluating controllers, ask:

- Can the operator program multiple bend steps easily?
- Can the system store repeat jobs?
- Does it support angle correction or bending compensation?
- Is the screen and language suitable for your operators?
- Can files be transferred by USB, network, or other methods?
- Is local service or remote support available?

For buyers new to CNC bending, training and documentation are important. A machine with advanced control can still underperform if operators are not prepared. Ask whether the supplier provides operation videos, manuals, remote guidance, or installation support.

## 5. Compare Backgauge and Axis Configuration

The backgauge positions the sheet before bending. For simple parts, a standard backgauge may be enough. For complex parts or higher repeatability, additional axes can improve flexibility and reduce manual adjustment.

Common questions include:

- How many backgauge axes are needed?
- Does the part require different flange depths?
- Is high repeatability important?
- Will operators produce many small batches?
- Are there special part shapes that require custom support?

More axes do not automatically mean a better investment. They should match the production need. If the buyer’s work is simple and repetitive, a practical configuration may deliver better ROI than an overly complex system. If the buyer produces precision cabinets, electrical enclosures, or varied custom parts, a higher-level configuration may reduce setup time and operator dependence.

## 6. Do Not Ignore Tooling

Tooling strongly affects bend quality. Punches, dies, clamping systems, segmented tools, and special tooling should be selected according to part geometry and material. Many buyers focus on the machine frame and controller but forget tooling until production begins.

Ask these questions early:

- What standard tooling is included?
- Does the tooling match your material thickness and bend radius?
- Do you need segmented tooling for boxes or enclosures?
- Is quick clamping needed to reduce setup time?
- Can replacement tooling be supplied later?
- Are special punches or dies required for your parts?

If the workpiece has tall flanges, narrow channels, small return bends, or box shapes, tooling clearance matters. A supplier should review part drawings before confirming tooling. RONGWIN-style product pages should include tooling as part of the RFQ process, not as an afterthought.

## 7. Match the Machine to Your Industry Application

A press brake used for general sheet metal fabrication may need versatility. A machine for cabinet production may need efficient tooling changes and repeatable accuracy. A construction parts supplier may need longer bending length and durable performance. An elevator or enclosure manufacturer may care about surface quality, panel precision, and part consistency.

Typical application groups include:

- Metal fabrication workshops.
- Furniture and cabinet manufacturing.
- Building and construction parts.
- Elevator, electrical cabinet, and enclosure production.
- Automotive and machinery components.
- Custom metal product manufacturing.

When writing an inquiry, describe your application. “We bend 2 mm stainless steel electrical cabinets” is much more useful than “I need a press brake.” The more specific the application, the better the supplier can recommend tonnage, tooling, controller, and accessories.

## 8. Review Factory Testing and Inspection

For overseas buyers, factory testing and shipment inspection are essential trust signals. You may not be able to visit the factory before shipment, so the supplier should provide clear evidence that the machine was produced, inspected, tested, and packed properly.

Recommended inspection items include:

- Machine appearance and nameplate.
- Electrical cabinet and wiring check.
- Hydraulic system inspection if applicable.
- Controller and backgauge operation.
- Ram movement and repeatability check.
- Trial bending video with sample material.
- Safety system check.
- Packing and loading photos.

Ask whether the supplier can provide a testing video and inspection report before shipment. This does not replace professional third-party inspection if required, but it improves transparency and reduces misunderstandings.

## 9. Prepare for Installation Before the Machine Arrives

Installation preparation should begin before shipping. Buyers should confirm factory space, unloading equipment, power supply, foundation requirements, operator training, and tooling storage.

Check:

- Machine dimensions and weight.
- Door height and unloading route.
- Crane or forklift capacity.
- Electrical voltage and frequency.
- Air or hydraulic requirements if any.
- Operator and maintenance staff availability.
- Required tooling and spare parts.

Good preparation reduces downtime after delivery. For international buyers, remote guidance can be useful, but the buyer should still prepare the site properly. If the machine arrives before the foundation, power, or lifting equipment is ready, the project can be delayed even if the machine itself is correct.

## 10. Evaluate Supplier Support, Not Only Price

Press brakes are long-term production assets. The supplier should be able to support machine selection, documentation, spare parts, troubleshooting, and upgrades. A lower initial price may not be the best value if the buyer cannot get help after delivery.

Before choosing a supplier, ask:

- Does the supplier understand my application?
- Can they explain the machine configuration clearly?
- Do they provide testing evidence before shipment?
- Are spare parts available?
- Can they support installation and operation guidance?
- Do they have export experience for my region?
- Can they help with future tooling or upgrade needs?

## FAQ

### What is the most important factor when choosing a press brake?

The most important factor is whether the machine matches your real parts. Tonnage, bending length, tooling, controller, and backgauge should be selected based on material, thickness, geometry, accuracy, and production volume.

### Is a higher tonnage press brake always better?

No. Higher tonnage can provide more capacity, but it also increases cost and may be unnecessary for light sheet metal work. The correct tonnage should be calculated around material, thickness, length, and die opening.

### Should I choose a CNC press brake?

Choose CNC when you need repeatability, multiple part programs, frequent job changes, better control, and easier operation. For simple low-volume work, a simpler machine may be enough.

### What should I ask for before shipment?

Ask for testing video, inspection report, machine photos, packaging photos, loading photos, manuals, and spare parts list. If the order is large or critical, consider third-party inspection.

### Can one supplier provide tooling and spare parts?

A strong supplier should help with tooling selection and spare parts planning. This is important for long-term production and service.

## Conclusion

A press brake should be chosen through a practical engineering and purchasing process, not only by model number or price. Start with your parts, confirm material and thickness, calculate tonnage, choose bending length, review tooling, compare controller options, and verify supplier support. For overseas buyers, factory testing, shipment inspection, and installation preparation are also essential.
